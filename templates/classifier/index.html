How it works

Automated Political Stance Identification (APSI)

APSI analyzes political language in text and estimates stance across three research-grounded dimensions: economic left–right, support for liberal democracy, and populism versus pluralism.

Quick summary

This tool evaluates whether your text entails (supports) a set of theory-driven political statements, then aggregates these signals into 0–10 scores with confidence estimates.

Analyze a text FAQ

What the tool does

APSI is designed for transparent, reproducible political text analysis. It outputs quantitative stance scores for texts, not labels for people. The results are probabilistic signals based on patterns in political language.

✅ Designed for

Research, journalism, education, and exploratory analysis of political communication (e.g., speeches, manifestos, posts, articles).

  • Compare stances across texts or time
  • Inspect rhetoric across countries or actors
  • Support transparent measurement with theory-based categories
🚫 Not designed for
  • Profiling individuals
  • Hiring, screening, or surveillance
  • Determining “true beliefs” or intent
  • High-stakes decisions

The dimensions we measure

Scores range from 0 to 10. Low and high values represent different ends of each dimension. Interpret them as signals in the text—not definitive judgments.

Economic ideology

Economic Left–Right

0 (Left): more redistribution, regulation, welfare, public services.

10 (Right): lower taxes, privatization, deregulation, smaller state.

Democratic values

Support for Liberal Democracy

0: weak support for democratic institutions, rule of law, civil liberties.

10: strong support for pluralism, constitutional checks, rights and liberties.

Rhetorical style

Populism–Pluralism

0 (Pluralism): inclusive, institutional, consensus-oriented framing.

10 (Populism): “the people vs. the elite”, anti-establishment rhetoric.

How the analysis works

APSI uses a Natural Language Inference (NLI) approach. In practice, the model checks whether your text supports a set of political statements (hypotheses) derived from expert-validated political frameworks.

In plain terms: we ask “Does this text imply X?” for multiple theory-based statements, then aggregate the answers into scores.
Step 1

Turn theory into hypotheses

Each dimension is represented by multiple short statements (e.g., left vs. right economic positions), grounded in established codebooks and expert survey frameworks.

Step 2

Compute entailment probabilities

For each hypothesis, the model estimates how strongly the input text entails (supports) that statement. These probabilities are then combined to form an overall stance score.

Step 3

Transform signals into 0–10 scores

Scores are normalized to a 0–10 scale to make dimensions comparable and easy to interpret. This supports cross-text and cross-country comparisons.

The three approaches you may see in results

Depending on selected options, APSI can score your text using complementary methods. These help triangulate results and make assumptions visible.

Approach A

Direct classification

A single-step estimate of stance along each dimension. Useful for quick signals and baseline comparison.

Approach B

Hypothesis-based scoring

The model evaluates multiple “left” and “right” (or “pluralist” and “populist”) hypotheses, averages them, takes differences, and maps them onto a 0–10 scale.

Approach C

Response-based scoring

The model compares the text to anchored category descriptions (e.g., Left = 0, Center = 5, Right = 10), normalizes probabilities, and computes a weighted average score.

What confidence means

Confidence reflects how internally consistent the model’s signals were for the given text. It is not the same as “accuracy” or “truth.”

High confidence

Clear signals detected

The text contains strong, consistent cues aligned with the hypotheses or category anchors.

Low confidence

Ambiguous or mixed signals

The text is short, balanced, context-dependent, sarcastic, or not strongly related to the dimension.

Validation and reliability

APSI is designed to be validated against human-coded political texts (e.g., manifestos and political leaders’ communication), and continuously improved through expert review and refinement of hypotheses.

Important: like all text-based stance detection, results can vary with domain, language, and wording. Validation is an ongoing process—especially for multilingual use.

Limitations

Political language is nuanced and context-dependent. The tool may misread rhetorical strategies and indirect cues. Treat outputs as indicators, not final judgments.

Common failure cases

  • Sarcasm, irony, quoting opponents
  • Very short or highly technical texts
  • Context missing (who speaks, when, where)
  • Mixed or strategic language

Bias and generalization

  • Training data may encode historical or regional biases
  • Performance may vary across countries and languages
  • Small wording changes can shift model signals

Ethical use

APSI is intended for informational and research purposes. It should not be used for high-stakes decisions that affect individuals’ rights, opportunities, or access to services.

Do not use APSI for: profiling people, surveillance, hiring or screening, or any automated decision-making that impacts individuals.

FAQ

Quick answers to common questions about interpretation and reliability.

Does APSI “know” what the author believes?

No. APSI analyzes the language in the text and estimates how strongly it aligns with certain political dimensions. It does not infer personal beliefs, intentions, or identity.

Why do results change when I rewrite a sentence?

Stance detection is sensitive to wording. Small changes can strengthen or weaken cues that the model uses to evaluate hypotheses or category anchors.

Can the tool detect sarcasm or quotes?

Sometimes, but not reliably. Irony, sarcasm, and quoted statements are common sources of misclassification. If the text quotes opponents, consider analyzing only the author’s own statements.

What text length works best?

Longer passages with clear policy or institutional language tend to yield more stable scores. Very short texts (one sentence) are more uncertain.
